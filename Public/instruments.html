History

The nylon-string guitar, also called the classical guitar, evolved from the lute, a popular stringed instrument during the Rennaisance period in Europe. The lute had between six and thirteen courses of doubled nylon strings. As luthier technology improved, the need to double up strings to produce resonant sounds was no longer, and what we know as the classical guitar emerged. It was smaller than the lute, had a different shape, had six distinct nylon strings, and could more easily be played with the fingers rather than a plectrum. This new classical guitar lent itself well to fast and intricate playing, so it became very popular in Spanish music, especially flamenco. In the 19th century, the classical guitar gained popularity and classical musicians and composers began to make use of it. Classical music's popularity helped the nylon string guitar become popular, and today the classical guitar is most commonly used in classical, jazz, and latin music.
